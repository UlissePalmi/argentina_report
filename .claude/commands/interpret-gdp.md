## Conceptual framework

### Y = C + I + G + NX

| Component | Spanish name | Share of GDP | What to watch |
|---|---|---|---|
| C | Consumo Privado | ~65-70% | Real wage recovery, consumer confidence |
| I | Inversión (FBCF) | volatile | Business confidence, exchange rate stability |
| G | Consumo Público | declining | Fiscal surplus path, Milei's spending cuts |

## Interpreting each component

### C — Private Consumption
**How Can C Grow**
- Real Wage Growth
- Credit Expansion
- Drawing Down Savings 

C rising is only sustainable if driven by real wage gains, not credit expansion or import-financed consumption. If C is rising while I is falling, flag whether consumption is being fed by imports (consumer goods share rising).

**Thresholds:**
- C > 5% YoY: strong domestic demand — watch import spillover
- C 0–3% YoY: modest recovery
- C negative: recession signal for the domestic economy

### I — Investment (FBCF)
Investment is the canary in the coal mine — falls first when uncertainty rises, recovers last when stability returns. Particularly sensitive to the reserve/exchange rate situation: if net reserves are near zero and devaluation risk is elevated, firms delay capex.

**Thresholds:**
- I > 10% YoY: strong investment cycle — very positive
- I 0–10% YoY: moderate — note which sectors are driving it
- I negative: flag prominently — connect to macro context (reserves, FX, credit)
- I < –5% YoY: serious warning — investment collapse

### G — Government Consumption
Under Milei, G is structurally declining as part of fiscal consolidation. A falling G is **intentional** — do not flag it as negative in isolation. It becomes a problem only if it falls so fast that it triggers a recession without C and NX compensating.

**Thresholds:**
- G 0% to –3% YoY: on track with fiscal plan
- G < –5% YoY: aggressive cutting — watch for social/political pushback
- G turning positive: fiscal slippage — flag as program risk

### NX — Net Exports
NX in the GDP accounts is a **volume measure** (real terms), not a dollar flow. Do not conflate with the trade balance in the current account, which is in dollars. Always note harvest seasonality: Q2 is peak soy inflow — a strong NX in Q2 does not mean the full year will be strong.

**Import growth interpretation:**
- Imports rising with GDP rising: normal, demand-driven
- Imports rising faster than GDP: real exchange rate appreciation making imports cheap — flag as competitiveness concern
- Imports falling with GDP falling: recessionary compression, not a sign of strength

## The two-speed economy warning

If agriculture and mining are driving the headline but manufacturing and commerce are contracting, flag this explicitly as **commodity-driven growth masking domestic weakness**. Reference pattern: January 2026 EMAE +1.9% YoY, but manufacturing –2.6%, commerce –3.2%, with all growth from agriculture +25.1% and mining +9.6%.

---

## The dollar link — always make this explicit

- **C rising + I falling:** Is consumption being fed by imports? If yes, households are consuming imported goods, draining dollars without building productive capacity. Unsustainable if net reserves are thin.
- **NX positive but CA negative:** Goods surplus being offset by services deficit and income outflows (debt interest). The GDP measure flatters the true external position — note this distinction.
- **Agriculture driving growth:** Positive for near-term dollar inflows but seasonal. Note whether it is structural (Vaca Muerta) or cyclical (harvest recovery).

## Report structure — answer in this order

1. **Headline** — What is YoY GDP growth? Accelerating or decelerating vs the prior quarter?

2. **Composition** — What is driving it? Is it C, I, G, or NX? Is the mix healthy or concerning?

3. **Two-speed check** — Does the component breakdown confirm broad-based growth or reveal domestic weakness hidden in the headline?

4. **Forward signal** — What does investment growth (or contraction) tell us about the next 2–4 quarters?

5. **Dollar link** — Is the growth composition helping or hurting the external position?
