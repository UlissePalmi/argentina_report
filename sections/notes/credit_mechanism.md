# Argentina Credit Mechanism: The Full Picture

This note explains the structural chain behind Argentina's consumer credit boom.
It must be narrated in the financing report's credit expansion section.

---

## 1. The Setup — Trapped Pesos

Because of a long economic crisis, normal lending (mortgages, business loans) had mostly stopped.
Citizens kept trillions of pesos in bank accounts to earn interest and survive high inflation.
Banks were sitting on a massive pile of pesos with nowhere safe to lend in the private sector.

## 2. The Trap — The Central Bank as Money Printer

To prevent banks from dropping rates to zero — which would have triggered a dollar run — the government
had to absorb those trapped pesos.

- **Old system:** The Central Bank borrowed pesos from banks (Leliqs / Pases) and paid up to 133% interest.
- **Problem:** The Central Bank doesn't collect taxes. To pay that interest, it printed trillions of pesos → hyperinflation.

## 3. The Fix (mid-2024) — Debt Swap

Milei's government moved the quasi-fiscal debt off the Central Bank's balance sheet.

- **The swap:** Central Bank debt erased; replaced with Treasury-issued notes (LEFIs, LECAPs).
- **New mechanic:** Banks park pesos in Treasury bonds. Interest is paid from tax revenue, not money printing.
- **Consequence:** To afford this massive new interest bill, the government was forced to run a fiscal surplus through brutal budget cuts.

## 4. The Rate Cut — Stockpiling Dollars

Historically, exorbitant peso rates were needed to "bribe" savers into not buying dollars.

- **Reserve build-up:** BCRA aggressively bought dollars from booming agri/energy export sectors.
- **Rate cut:** With a strong dollar stockpile to defend the currency, BCRA cut the benchmark rate from ~133% down to 20%.

## 5. The Credit Boom — Banks Lend, Citizens Borrow

- **Banks shift:** At 20%, parking money in Treasury bonds is no longer highly profitable → banks are forced to lend to the private sector again.
- **Households borrow:** Lower rates make credit cheaper. Workers whose real wages have shrunk are aggressively taking out personal loans and using credit cards to maintain their standard of living.
- **The data:** This perfect storm — banks needing to lend, struggling citizens needing to borrow — caused the **64.0% spike in real consumer credit**.

---

## Framing for the Report

The credit boom is **not a sign of prosperity**. It is households using cheap credit to bridge a consumption gap
created by real wage compression. This creates a potential vulnerability: if wages don't recover,
debt servicing becomes a growing burden on already-strained household balance sheets.

The structural chain to communicate:
> Monetary reform → quasi-fiscal debt eliminated → rate normalization → banks forced into private lending → households borrowing to survive
